Radiation Biology



Radiation biology is the study of the ways in which radiation affects biological tissue and organisms. 


Early researchers sought reliable ways to predict and measure the effects of radiation on living tissues. This was particularly important in attempts to compare different methods of administration, as in the early saturation vs. fractionation controversy. Throughout the century, radiation biologists have worked in active cooperation with clinicians to refine our knowledge of the varying effects of radiation on the living body.  Today both in vitro and in vivo observations have enriched our understanding of these processes, and point to a future which includes cellular and genetic promise in radiation response modifiers and increasing precision in applying radiation to disease. Current trends in radiation biology include studies in radiation hormesis (simply stated: a little radiation may be good for you).


Some of the first radiation biology observations were by Bergonie and Tribondeau in 1906. These French researchers' work on rodent testes produced a widely promulgated "law", stated as: sensitivity to radiation varies directly with reproductive capacity and inversely with differentiation (maturity). Dessauer's seven laws of homogeneous irradiation in 1921 expanded these concepts, further modified by Ancel and Vitemburger in 1925 to include variable times in the appearance of radiation effects.
