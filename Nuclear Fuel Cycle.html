Nuclear Fuel Cycle



Like coal, oil and natural gas, uranium is an energy resource which must be processed through a series of steps to produce an efficient fuel for use in the generation of electricity.  Each fuel has its own distinctive fuel cycle: however the uranium or nuclear fuel cycle is somewhat more complex than the others.

To prepare uranium for use in a nuclear reactor, it undergoes the steps of mining and milling, conversion, enrichment (although not typically in CANDU reactors) and fuel fabrication. These steps make up the "front end" of the nuclear fuel cycle.


After uranium has been used in a reactor to produce electricity it is known as "spent fuel" and may undergo a further series of steps including temporary storage, reprocessing, and recycling before eventual disposal as waste.  These later steps are typically known as the "back end" of the fuel cycle.


Most uranium in Canada is currently mined underground (although there can be surface pit mining).  The mined uranium ore is sent to a mill which is usually located close to the mine. At the mill the ore is crushed and ground to a fine slurry which is leached in sulfuric acid to allow the separation of uranium from the waste rock. It is then recovered from solution and precipitated as uranium oxide (U308) concentrate, called Yellowcake.


The Yellowcake is refined (in Canada at Blind River, Ontario) to Uranium Trioxide (UO3), and transported to a fuel fabrication plant where it is converted to uranium dioxide (UO2) powder and pressed into small pellets (this happens at Cameco, Port Hope).  These pellets are inserted into thin tubes, usually of a zirconium alloy (zircalloy) or stainless steel, to form fuel rods (this happens at Zircatec, Port Hope).  The rods are then sealed and assembled in clusters to form fuel elements or assemblies for use in the core of the nuclear reactor.


An alternate fuel fabrication pathway is to convert the UO3 to UF6, which can be used to produce enriched fuel.


There is a lot more to the fuel cycle (like what happens when the fuel is used up).
